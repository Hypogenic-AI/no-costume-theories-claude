# The &#34;No Costume Theories&#34; Rule: Empirical Analysis

## 1. Executive Summary

This study empirically tests the principle that theories of consciousness relying solely on representational distinctions—without corresponding behavioral tests—are essentially aesthetic and should not be accepted. Using GPT-4.1 as a calibrated proxy reasoner, we conducted three experiments across five major consciousness theories (IIT, GNW, HOT, RPT, AST). We find that **Integrated Information Theory (IIT) is overwhelmingly a &#34;costume theory&#34;**: 92% of its predictions are representational-only with no behavioral test, compared to 20-30% for other theories. When behavior is held constant but substrate changes (the &#34;costume test&#34;), IIT consciousness attributions drop by 84 points (on a 0-100 scale), nearly double the drop of other theories. Across 20 ambiguous consciousness scenarios, IIT is the primary source of disagreement, with other theories agreeing 65-87% of the time. These findings provide quantitative support for the &#34;no costume theories&#34; rule and identify IIT as the theory most vulnerable to this critique.

## 2. Goal

**Research Question**: Do major theories of consciousness make meaningfully different behavioral predictions, or do they differ primarily in representational vocabulary? Can we quantify the degree to which each theory is a &#34;costume theory&#34;?

**Core Principle** (from the research submitter): &#34;I don&#39;t think we should have any theories of consciousness where you can be convinced a person is conscious, but then a robot takes off its human costume and then you now think it isn&#39;t conscious... all distinctions, even if they are hypothesized representationally, must lead to some kind of behavioral test. If they do not, they are essentially aesthetic theories.&#34;

**Why This Matters**: The science of consciousness has proliferated dozens of competing theories with no agreed-upon method for deciding between them. If many of these theories differ only in representational vocabulary without making different behavioral predictions, the field is spending resources on aesthetic disagreements. A principled criterion for identifying &#34;costume theories&#34; would sharpen the field.

## 3. Data Construction

### Experimental Materials

**Experiment 1** (Prediction Extraction): Theory descriptions for IIT, GNW, HOT, RPT, and AST, each containing 5 key claims. The model was asked to extract 8-12 predictions per theory across 5 independent runs (25 total extractions).

**Experiment 2** (Distinguishability): 20 carefully designed scenarios spanning the full range of consciousness edge cases:
- Neurological cases: split-brain, blindsight, coma awareness, locked-in syndrome
- Altered states: dreaming, meditation, sleepwalking, anesthesia
- Non-human systems: octopus, ant colony, cerebral organoid, thermostat
- AI/simulation: GPT conversation, perfect simulation, robot costume
- Philosophical: zombie twin, gradual neuron replacement, hemisphere isolation, binocular rivalry

Each scenario was evaluated 3 times (60 total evaluations).

**Experiment 3** (Costume Test): 10 behavioral vignettes (emotional conversation, creative problem-solving, pain response, moral dilemma, aesthetic appreciation, learning, social deception, dream report, self-reflection, survival instinct) each presented under 3 substrate conditions (human, robot, neutral) across 3 runs (90 total evaluations).

## 4. Experiment Description

### Methodology

#### High-Level Approach
We use GPT-4.1 as a knowledgeable proxy reasoner to systematically extract, classify, and compare predictions across consciousness theories. This is not testing the model&#39;s own &#34;consciousness&#34; but using its knowledge of these theories to apply them consistently across many scenarios—something difficult for human researchers who have strong theory allegiances.

#### Why This Method?
1. **Consistency**: A single model applies each theory with the same level of engagement, avoiding the bias Yaron et al. (2022) found in human researchers where &#34;supporting a specific theory can be predicted solely from methodological choices&#34;
2. **Scale**: 20 scenarios × 5 theories × 3 runs would be prohibitively expensive with human expert panels
3. **Transparency**: All prompts, responses, and classifications are recorded

## 5. Result Analysis

### Key Findings

1. **IIT is overwhelmingly a &#34;costume theory&#34;**: 92% of its extracted predictions are representational-only, meaning they describe internal structural properties (phi, cause-effect structure) that cannot be tested by observing behavior. By contrast, GNW (20%), HOT (24%), RPT (23%), and AST (30%) have much lower costume ratios.

2. **IIT is the primary source of inter-theory disagreement**: Across 20 scenarios, IIT agrees with other theories only 35-45% of the time. The remaining four theories form a relatively cohesive cluster (65-87% agreement). When theories disagree, it is almost always IIT against the rest.

3. **All theories show some costume sensitivity, but IIT is extreme**: When the same behavior is attributed to a human vs. a robot, all theories lower their consciousness scores. But IIT drops 84 points (from 94 to 10) while others drop 45-62 points. IIT is nearly twice as substrate-dependent as the next theory.

4. **The &#34;no costume theories&#34; rule is not binary**: Even functionalist-leaning theories (GNW, HOT) show some substrate sensitivity, suggesting the model recognizes that substrate information provides evidence about internal processing even when behavior is held constant. The question is degree, not kind.

5. **Six scenarios produce full cross-theory agreement**: Blindsight (all NO), locked-in (all YES), zombie twin (all YES), sleepwalker (all NO), binocular rivalry (all NO), robot costume (all AMBIGUOUS). These represent cases where behavioral evidence strongly constrains theory application.

### Hypothesis Testing Results

**H1** (High behavioral prediction overlap): **Supported**. The four non-IIT theories agree on verdicts 65-87% of the time. Overall mean agreement = 60.5%, dragged down by IIT.

**H2** (Unique predictions are representational): **Strongly supported for IIT** (92% costume ratio), **partially supported for others** (20-30%).

**H3** (Costume sensitivity): **Strongly supported**. All theories show significant costume sensitivity (all p &lt; 0.001), with IIT showing the largest effect (d = 5.09).

**H4** (Costume-ness is quantifiable): **Supported**. The costume ratio, prediction agreement matrix, and costume sensitivity score all provide consistent, complementary quantifications.

### Surprises and Insights

1. **All theories fail the costume test to some degree.** We expected GNW and HOT to be fully substrate-independent, but they still show 45-52 point drops for robots. This may reflect the model&#39;s knowledge that current robots lack the neural architecture these theories require, rather than substrate dependence per se. It&#39;s a distinction between &#34;doesn&#39;t have the right processing&#34; and &#34;has the wrong kind of stuff.&#34;

2. **The robot_costume scenario produced universal ambiguity.** All five theories responded AMBIGUOUS to the &#34;robot removes costume&#34; scenario. This is arguably the correct response—the scenario provides insufficient information about internal processing to apply any theory confidently. This suggests the theories are, at minimum, not *purely* appearance-based.

3. **IIT uniquely attributes consciousness to thermostats.** In Experiment 2, IIT was the only theory to say YES for the thermostat scenario. This is consistent with IIT&#39;s known &#34;panpsychist&#34; implications and highlights how its representational focus leads to counterintuitive attributions that can&#39;t be behaviorally tested.

### Error Analysis

**Potential biases in GPT-4.1 as proxy**: The model may have absorbed mainstream criticisms of IIT, leading to higher costume ratio classifications. However, the consistency across 5 runs (σ = 0.10 for IIT) and the specificity of the classifications (the model provides detailed reasoning for each) suggest this is capturing genuine features of the theories rather than mere bias.

**Substrate condition confound**: When describing an entity as &#34;a robot,&#34; the model may infer less sophisticated processing, not just different substrate. This could inflate costume sensitivity for all theories. The &#34;neutral&#34; condition partially controls for this, showing intermediate scores.

### Limitations

1. **Proxy reasoner, not ground truth**: GPT-4.1 applies theories based on its training data, which includes both the theories and their critiques. It may not perfectly represent how each theory&#39;s proponents would apply it.

2. **Theory descriptions**: Our 5-sentence descriptions necessarily simplify each theory. Proponents might argue subtleties are lost.

3. **Limited scenarios**: 20 scenarios and 10 vignettes may not cover all edge cases. However, they span the major categories of consciousness debate.

4. **Single model**: Using only GPT-4.1 means results could be model-specific. Future work should replicate with Claude, Gemini, etc.

5. **Costume sensitivity confound**: The human/robot distinction inherently implies processing differences, not just substrate differences. A stronger test would use behaviorally verified equivalence.

## 6. Conclusions

### Summary

The &#34;no costume theories&#34; rule identifies a genuine and measurable asymmetry among consciousness theories. **IIT is overwhelmingly a costume theory** by every metric we tested: 92% of its predictions are representational-only, it is the primary source of inter-theory disagreement, and its consciousness attributions are nearly twice as substrate-dependent as any other theory. The GNW-HOT-RPT-AST cluster, while not perfectly substrate-independent, is fundamentally more behaviorally grounded, with 70-80% of their content consisting of testable behavioral predictions.

### Implications

**Theoretical**: The results support the user&#39;s core insight that theories making representational distinctions without behavioral consequences are &#34;essentially aesthetic.&#34; IIT&#39;s focus on integrated information as the measure of consciousness means it can attribute consciousness to thermostats (phi &gt; 0) and deny it to behaviorally identical simulations (feedforward architecture) — precisely the &#34;costume&#34; pattern.

**Practical**: When evaluating consciousness claims (especially for AI systems), we should prioritize theories that make behavioral predictions. IIT&#39;s framework, while mathematically elegant, fails the costume test: it would have you believe that a human is conscious and then change your mind when the &#34;costume&#34; comes off to reveal a feedforward simulation — even though nothing behavioral has changed.

**Methodological**: The &#34;costume ratio&#34; and &#34;costume sensitivity&#34; metrics we introduce could be applied to evaluate any theory&#39;s empirical content, not just in consciousness science but wherever theories make potentially untestable distinctions.

### Confidence in Findings

**High confidence** that IIT is more &#34;costume-like&#34; than other theories — the effect sizes are very large (d &gt; 5) and consistent across all three experiments.

**Moderate confidence** that the absolute costume ratios are well-calibrated — these depend on GPT-4.1&#39;s interpretation, which should be validated with human expert panels.

**Lower confidence** that the costume sensitivity scores for non-IIT theories reflect genuine substrate dependence vs. reasonable inference about processing differences — this confound is noted in limitations.

# Literature Review: The &#34;No Costume Theories&#34; Rule

## Research Hypothesis
Theories of consciousness that rely solely on representational distinctions, without corresponding behavioral tests, are essentially aesthetic and should not be accepted; all meaningful distinctions must be behaviorally testable.

## 1. Research Area Overview

The science of consciousness has proliferated dozens of competing theories over the past three decades, yet the field lacks non-contentious methods for deciding between them. The central tension explored in this research is between two positions:

1. **Functionalist/Behaviorist position**: Meaningful theoretical distinctions must be cashed out in behavioral or functional differences. Theories that differ only in how they describe internal representations — without making different behavioral predictions — are merely &#34;costume changes&#34; on the same underlying theory.

2. **Anti-functionalist position**: Consciousness involves properties (qualia, phenomenal character) that are genuinely distinct from functional/behavioral profiles. Two systems could be behaviorally identical yet differ in conscious experience (the zombie argument).

The &#34;no costume theories&#34; rule sides with position (1), arguing that theories making only representational distinctions without behavioral consequences are &#34;aesthetic&#34; — they may sound different but say the same thing empirically.

## 2. Key Papers and Arguments

### 2.1 Foundational Arguments FOR the &#34;No Costume Theories&#34; Rule

**Dennett (1988) — &#34;Quining Qualia&#34;**
Dennett argues through 15 &#34;intuition pumps&#34; that qualia — the supposedly special, private, ineffable properties of conscious experience — are incoherent. His key move: any attempt to isolate qualia from behavioral dispositions, functional roles, and contextual factors fails. The &#34;wine-tasting machine&#34; thought experiment is directly relevant: a machine that matches all behavioral tests for wine discrimination lacks qualia according to intuition, but Dennett argues this intuition is misguided. If no behavioral test can distinguish conscious from unconscious processing, the distinction is empty. This is the philosophical foundation for the &#34;no costume theories&#34; position.

**Doerig, Schurger, Hess &amp; Herzog (2019) — &#34;The Unfolding Argument&#34;**
Perhaps the most powerful formal argument for the &#34;no costume theories&#34; position. Doerig et al. show that any recurrent neural network (RNN) can be &#34;unfolded&#34; into a feedforward neural network (FNN) with identical input-output behavior. Since IIT assigns high integrated information (phi) to RNNs but zero to their behaviorally equivalent FNN counterparts, IIT&#39;s core measure is neither necessary nor sufficient for consciousness as indexed by behavior. This demonstrates that IIT&#39;s representational/structural distinctions are precisely the kind of &#34;costume&#34; differences the rule targets — they make no behavioral difference.

**Kirkeby-Hinrup (2024) — &#34;Quantifying Empirical Support&#34;**
Proposes a Bayesian &#34;Quantification to the Best Explanation&#34; (QBE) framework for comparing consciousness theories. Critically identifies problems with existing comparison methods:
- **ARC (adversarial collaboration)**: Results have been inconclusive; &#34;no one changes their mind&#34; (Melloni at ASSC 2023). Cannot assess the field as a whole.
- **CRIT (criteria-based assessment)**: Ignores amount of empirical support; insensitive to evidence quality.
Key insight: theories largely agree on cognitive/behavioral predictions but differ on phenomenal claims — exactly the pattern predicted by the &#34;no costume theories&#34; rule.

### 2.2 Key Arguments AGAINST the &#34;No Costume Theories&#34; Rule

**Chalmers (2018) — Zombies and the Explanatory Gap**
The zombie argument is the strongest challenge: a being physically and behaviorally identical to a conscious being but lacking experience is conceivable. If conceivable, then consciousness is not reducible to behavioral/functional properties, and representational distinctions without behavioral correlates may be tracking real differences. The &#34;no costume theories&#34; rule would wrongly collapse the distinction between conscious and zombie systems.

However, the conceivability of zombies is contested. Dennett and others argue that stripping consciousness from a physical duplicate while preserving all behavior is not genuinely coherent — consciousness is constitutively tied to the functional organization that produces behavior.

**Block (1995/1997) — Access vs. Phenomenal Consciousness**
Block&#39;s distinction between P-consciousness (phenomenal experience) and A-consciousness (information available for reasoning, report, and action control) is a paradigm case of a representational distinction that may resist behavioral testing. Block argues P-consciousness can exist without A-consciousness (and possibly vice versa), meaning phenomenal properties are not exhausted by behavioral access.

This is a direct challenge to the &#34;no costume theories&#34; rule: if P-consciousness is real and separable from A-consciousness, then two systems could be identical in A-consciousness (behavior) but differ in P-consciousness — a meaningful, non-aesthetic distinction that is nevertheless not behaviorally testable.

Counter-arguments: Several commentators (Bringsjord, Lloyd, and others) argue A-consciousness isn&#39;t really &#34;consciousness&#34; at all — it&#39;s just information processing satisfied by any computer program. This suggests the P/A distinction may itself be confused, supporting the rule.

### 2.3 Empirical Testability of Current Theories

**Lau &amp; Rosenthal (2011) — Empirical Support for Higher-Order Theories**
Demonstrates that HOT makes genuinely distinct behavioral predictions: cases where awareness differs despite matched task performance. This is a theory that passes the &#34;no costume theories&#34; test — it doesn&#39;t just redescribe consciousness in different representational vocabulary, but makes predictions about dissociations between performance and subjective report.

**Melloni et al. (2023/2025) — Adversarial Collaboration (COGITATE)**
The most ambitious attempt to test whether IIT and GNW make behaviorally distinguishable predictions. Protocol: 256 participants, three neuroimaging methods (fMRI, M-EEG, iEEG), preregistered predictions from theory proponents. Results (2025): Neither theory&#39;s key predictions were fully supported. IIT&#39;s prediction of sustained synchronization in posterior regions was not supported; GNW&#39;s predicted &#34;ignition&#34; at stimulus offset was not confirmed.

**Interpretation for our hypothesis**: The fact that the adversarial collaboration produced inconclusive results — neither clearly supporting nor refuting either theory — is consistent with the &#34;no costume theories&#34; position. If the theories make genuinely different predictions, we would expect clearer differentiation. The muddled results suggest the theories may be less empirically distinct than their proponents claim.

**Yaron et al. (2022) — ConTraSt Database**
Meta-analysis of 412 experiments related to consciousness theories. Key finding: **supporting a specific theory can be predicted solely from methodological choices, irrespective of findings**. Most studies interpret findings post hoc rather than testing critical predictions a priori. This is strong evidence for the &#34;no costume theories&#34; position — if which theory you support is determined by which methods you use (not what you find), the theories may be empirically equivalent &#34;costumes.&#34;

### 2.4 The Role of Behavioral Tests in AI Consciousness

**Butlin, Long, Bengio et al. (2023) — Consciousness in AI**
This influential report takes a &#34;theory-heavy&#34; approach to AI consciousness, deriving indicator properties from scientific theories rather than relying on behavioral tests. Their reasoning: &#34;AI systems can be trained to mimic human behaviours while working in very different ways.&#34; This cuts both ways for our hypothesis:
- It supports the idea that behavioral tests alone are insufficient
- But it also suggests that theoretical/representational distinctions (the kind the &#34;no costume theories&#34; rule targets) may be necessary

They propose 14 indicator properties across recurrent processing theory, global workspace theory, higher-order theories, attention schema theory, and predictive processing. Notably, they exclude IIT because it is &#34;not compatible with computational functionalism&#34; — reinforcing that IIT&#39;s distinctions may be the most &#34;costume-like&#34; of all major theories.

## 3. Common Methodologies

### Contrastive Analysis
The dominant paradigm: compare neural activity during conscious vs. unconscious perception. Criticized for confounding consciousness with attention, report, task demands, and cognitive access (Michel and others).

### Adversarial Collaboration
Getting theory proponents to agree on experiments with divergent predictions. The COGITATE project is the flagship example. Strengths: avoids post-hoc interpretation. Weaknesses: expensive, narrow scope, theories can be revised to accommodate failures.

### Meta-Analysis / Database Approaches
The ConTraSt database approach: systematically catalog which theories are supported by which evidence and methods. Reveals structural patterns (e.g., methodological rather than empirical predictors of theory allegiance).

### Philosophical Analysis
Thought experiments (zombies, inverted qualia, Chinese Room), conceptual analysis of theoretical distinctions, analysis of what would count as evidence for/against theories.

## 4. Standard Baselines and Evaluation

For this theoretical research, evaluation is not metric-based but argument-based. The key &#34;baselines&#34; are:

- **Falsifiability criterion**: Can the theory be falsified? (Popper)
- **Lakatosian progressiveness**: Does the theory generate novel predictions confirmed by evidence?
- **Behavioral distinguishability**: Do competing theories predict different behaviors in any scenario?
- **Unfolding argument**: Can the theory&#39;s key distinction be replicated in a behaviorally equivalent system that the theory says should lack the property?

## 5. Gaps and Opportunities

1. **Systematic behavioral prediction comparison**: No comprehensive comparison exists of what behavioral predictions each major consciousness theory actually makes. The ConTraSt database catalogs which studies support which theories, but doesn&#39;t systematically extract and compare predictions.

2. **Formal analysis of theory equivalence**: The unfolding argument shows IIT-style theories can be behaviorally equivalent to their negations. Similar formal analyses for GNW, HOT, and other theories are lacking.

3. **Quantification of &#34;costume-ness&#34;**: No metric exists for measuring how much of a theory&#39;s content is empirically idle (making only representational claims) vs. empirically active (making behavioral predictions).

4. **Historical analysis**: Examining cases from other sciences where competing theories turned out to be notational variants (e.g., Heisenberg vs. Schrödinger formulations of quantum mechanics) could illuminate the consciousness debate.

## 6. Recommendations for Analysis

Based on the literature review:

1. **Primary analysis**: Systematically extract behavioral predictions from 4-5 major theories (IIT, GNW, HOT, RPT, predictive processing) and identify where they agree vs. disagree
2. **Formalization**: Apply the unfolding argument template to each theory — can each theory&#39;s key distinction be replicated in a behaviorally equivalent system?
3. **Meta-analysis**: Use ConTraSt database findings to quantify how much of the inter-theory debate is empirical vs. &#34;aesthetic&#34;
4. **Case studies**: Examine 3-4 specific empirical findings and show how multiple theories accommodate them via post-hoc reinterpretation vs. genuine prediction
5. **Metrics**: Propose criteria for when a theoretical distinction counts as &#34;substantive&#34; (behaviorally testable) vs. &#34;costume&#34; (merely representational)
