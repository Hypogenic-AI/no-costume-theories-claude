# Literature Review: The "No Costume Theories" Rule

## Research Hypothesis
Theories of consciousness that rely solely on representational distinctions, without corresponding behavioral tests, are essentially aesthetic and should not be accepted; all meaningful distinctions must be behaviorally testable.

## 1. Research Area Overview

The science of consciousness has proliferated dozens of competing theories over the past three decades, yet the field lacks non-contentious methods for deciding between them. The central tension explored in this research is between two positions:

1. **Functionalist/Behaviorist position**: Meaningful theoretical distinctions must be cashed out in behavioral or functional differences. Theories that differ only in how they describe internal representations — without making different behavioral predictions — are merely "costume changes" on the same underlying theory.

2. **Anti-functionalist position**: Consciousness involves properties (qualia, phenomenal character) that are genuinely distinct from functional/behavioral profiles. Two systems could be behaviorally identical yet differ in conscious experience (the zombie argument).

The "no costume theories" rule sides with position (1), arguing that theories making only representational distinctions without behavioral consequences are "aesthetic" — they may sound different but say the same thing empirically.

## 2. Key Papers and Arguments

### 2.1 Foundational Arguments FOR the "No Costume Theories" Rule

**Dennett (1988) — "Quining Qualia"**
Dennett argues through 15 "intuition pumps" that qualia — the supposedly special, private, ineffable properties of conscious experience — are incoherent. His key move: any attempt to isolate qualia from behavioral dispositions, functional roles, and contextual factors fails. The "wine-tasting machine" thought experiment is directly relevant: a machine that matches all behavioral tests for wine discrimination lacks qualia according to intuition, but Dennett argues this intuition is misguided. If no behavioral test can distinguish conscious from unconscious processing, the distinction is empty. This is the philosophical foundation for the "no costume theories" position.

**Doerig, Schurger, Hess & Herzog (2019) — "The Unfolding Argument"**
Perhaps the most powerful formal argument for the "no costume theories" position. Doerig et al. show that any recurrent neural network (RNN) can be "unfolded" into a feedforward neural network (FNN) with identical input-output behavior. Since IIT assigns high integrated information (phi) to RNNs but zero to their behaviorally equivalent FNN counterparts, IIT's core measure is neither necessary nor sufficient for consciousness as indexed by behavior. This demonstrates that IIT's representational/structural distinctions are precisely the kind of "costume" differences the rule targets — they make no behavioral difference.

**Kirkeby-Hinrup (2024) — "Quantifying Empirical Support"**
Proposes a Bayesian "Quantification to the Best Explanation" (QBE) framework for comparing consciousness theories. Critically identifies problems with existing comparison methods:
- **ARC (adversarial collaboration)**: Results have been inconclusive; "no one changes their mind" (Melloni at ASSC 2023). Cannot assess the field as a whole.
- **CRIT (criteria-based assessment)**: Ignores amount of empirical support; insensitive to evidence quality.
Key insight: theories largely agree on cognitive/behavioral predictions but differ on phenomenal claims — exactly the pattern predicted by the "no costume theories" rule.

### 2.2 Key Arguments AGAINST the "No Costume Theories" Rule

**Chalmers (2018) — Zombies and the Explanatory Gap**
The zombie argument is the strongest challenge: a being physically and behaviorally identical to a conscious being but lacking experience is conceivable. If conceivable, then consciousness is not reducible to behavioral/functional properties, and representational distinctions without behavioral correlates may be tracking real differences. The "no costume theories" rule would wrongly collapse the distinction between conscious and zombie systems.

However, the conceivability of zombies is contested. Dennett and others argue that stripping consciousness from a physical duplicate while preserving all behavior is not genuinely coherent — consciousness is constitutively tied to the functional organization that produces behavior.

**Block (1995/1997) — Access vs. Phenomenal Consciousness**
Block's distinction between P-consciousness (phenomenal experience) and A-consciousness (information available for reasoning, report, and action control) is a paradigm case of a representational distinction that may resist behavioral testing. Block argues P-consciousness can exist without A-consciousness (and possibly vice versa), meaning phenomenal properties are not exhausted by behavioral access.

This is a direct challenge to the "no costume theories" rule: if P-consciousness is real and separable from A-consciousness, then two systems could be identical in A-consciousness (behavior) but differ in P-consciousness — a meaningful, non-aesthetic distinction that is nevertheless not behaviorally testable.

Counter-arguments: Several commentators (Bringsjord, Lloyd, and others) argue A-consciousness isn't really "consciousness" at all — it's just information processing satisfied by any computer program. This suggests the P/A distinction may itself be confused, supporting the rule.

### 2.3 Empirical Testability of Current Theories

**Lau & Rosenthal (2011) — Empirical Support for Higher-Order Theories**
Demonstrates that HOT makes genuinely distinct behavioral predictions: cases where awareness differs despite matched task performance. This is a theory that passes the "no costume theories" test — it doesn't just redescribe consciousness in different representational vocabulary, but makes predictions about dissociations between performance and subjective report.

**Melloni et al. (2023/2025) — Adversarial Collaboration (COGITATE)**
The most ambitious attempt to test whether IIT and GNW make behaviorally distinguishable predictions. Protocol: 256 participants, three neuroimaging methods (fMRI, M-EEG, iEEG), preregistered predictions from theory proponents. Results (2025): Neither theory's key predictions were fully supported. IIT's prediction of sustained synchronization in posterior regions was not supported; GNW's predicted "ignition" at stimulus offset was not confirmed.

**Interpretation for our hypothesis**: The fact that the adversarial collaboration produced inconclusive results — neither clearly supporting nor refuting either theory — is consistent with the "no costume theories" position. If the theories make genuinely different predictions, we would expect clearer differentiation. The muddled results suggest the theories may be less empirically distinct than their proponents claim.

**Yaron et al. (2022) — ConTraSt Database**
Meta-analysis of 412 experiments related to consciousness theories. Key finding: **supporting a specific theory can be predicted solely from methodological choices, irrespective of findings**. Most studies interpret findings post hoc rather than testing critical predictions a priori. This is strong evidence for the "no costume theories" position — if which theory you support is determined by which methods you use (not what you find), the theories may be empirically equivalent "costumes."

### 2.4 The Role of Behavioral Tests in AI Consciousness

**Butlin, Long, Bengio et al. (2023) — Consciousness in AI**
This influential report takes a "theory-heavy" approach to AI consciousness, deriving indicator properties from scientific theories rather than relying on behavioral tests. Their reasoning: "AI systems can be trained to mimic human behaviours while working in very different ways." This cuts both ways for our hypothesis:
- It supports the idea that behavioral tests alone are insufficient
- But it also suggests that theoretical/representational distinctions (the kind the "no costume theories" rule targets) may be necessary

They propose 14 indicator properties across recurrent processing theory, global workspace theory, higher-order theories, attention schema theory, and predictive processing. Notably, they exclude IIT because it is "not compatible with computational functionalism" — reinforcing that IIT's distinctions may be the most "costume-like" of all major theories.

## 3. Common Methodologies

### Contrastive Analysis
The dominant paradigm: compare neural activity during conscious vs. unconscious perception. Criticized for confounding consciousness with attention, report, task demands, and cognitive access (Michel and others).

### Adversarial Collaboration
Getting theory proponents to agree on experiments with divergent predictions. The COGITATE project is the flagship example. Strengths: avoids post-hoc interpretation. Weaknesses: expensive, narrow scope, theories can be revised to accommodate failures.

### Meta-Analysis / Database Approaches
The ConTraSt database approach: systematically catalog which theories are supported by which evidence and methods. Reveals structural patterns (e.g., methodological rather than empirical predictors of theory allegiance).

### Philosophical Analysis
Thought experiments (zombies, inverted qualia, Chinese Room), conceptual analysis of theoretical distinctions, analysis of what would count as evidence for/against theories.

## 4. Standard Baselines and Evaluation

For this theoretical research, evaluation is not metric-based but argument-based. The key "baselines" are:

- **Falsifiability criterion**: Can the theory be falsified? (Popper)
- **Lakatosian progressiveness**: Does the theory generate novel predictions confirmed by evidence?
- **Behavioral distinguishability**: Do competing theories predict different behaviors in any scenario?
- **Unfolding argument**: Can the theory's key distinction be replicated in a behaviorally equivalent system that the theory says should lack the property?

## 5. Gaps and Opportunities

1. **Systematic behavioral prediction comparison**: No comprehensive comparison exists of what behavioral predictions each major consciousness theory actually makes. The ConTraSt database catalogs which studies support which theories, but doesn't systematically extract and compare predictions.

2. **Formal analysis of theory equivalence**: The unfolding argument shows IIT-style theories can be behaviorally equivalent to their negations. Similar formal analyses for GNW, HOT, and other theories are lacking.

3. **Quantification of "costume-ness"**: No metric exists for measuring how much of a theory's content is empirically idle (making only representational claims) vs. empirically active (making behavioral predictions).

4. **Historical analysis**: Examining cases from other sciences where competing theories turned out to be notational variants (e.g., Heisenberg vs. Schrödinger formulations of quantum mechanics) could illuminate the consciousness debate.

## 6. Recommendations for Analysis

Based on the literature review:

1. **Primary analysis**: Systematically extract behavioral predictions from 4-5 major theories (IIT, GNW, HOT, RPT, predictive processing) and identify where they agree vs. disagree
2. **Formalization**: Apply the unfolding argument template to each theory — can each theory's key distinction be replicated in a behaviorally equivalent system?
3. **Meta-analysis**: Use ConTraSt database findings to quantify how much of the inter-theory debate is empirical vs. "aesthetic"
4. **Case studies**: Examine 3-4 specific empirical findings and show how multiple theories accommodate them via post-hoc reinterpretation vs. genuine prediction
5. **Metrics**: Propose criteria for when a theoretical distinction counts as "substantive" (behaviorally testable) vs. "costume" (merely representational)
